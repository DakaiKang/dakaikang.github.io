*Originally published at [Decentralized Thoughts](https://decentralizedthoughts.github.io/2024-08-24-hotstuff1/) on August 24, 2024. Co-authored with Suyash Gupta, Dahlia Malkhi, and Mohammad Sadoghi.*

This post examines three approaches to reducing network hops for finality in Byzantine Fault Tolerant (BFT) consensus through *speculation* — and the common challenge each runs into, the **prefix speculation dilemma**.

### HotStuff-1 speculation

The HotStuff family organizes consensus into sequential views with designated leaders, achieving linear communication per view and extending decisions by one position per view.

**HotStuff-1** introduces a two-hop latency reduction: validators may send execution responses after a single Quorum Certificate (QC) instead of two. When validators receive *n − f* responses, clients learn both that their request committed and the execution result.

A safety problem appears: when a validator speculatively applies a transaction at sequence position *s*, it must execute all preceding transactions as well. If conflicting proposals at different positions both obtain QCs but neither reaches consensus, validators may respond on earlier transactions that later abort, leading clients to incorrectly conclude those transactions committed.

Two resolutions:

- **Conservative rule:** validators speculate only after preceding transactions commit. Simple, but slower.
- **Permissive rule:** validators speculate freely but withhold responses on preceding transactions. Faster speculation, with out-of-order client responses.

### PBFT speculation

PBFT's traditional flow needs five network hops. The *tentative execution* optimization shaves one hop: validators apply requests tentatively upon collecting a prepare-certificate and send results to clients immediately.

The dilemma manifests differently in PBFT because PBFT allows out-of-order commits. A leader may propose position *s+1* before position *s* has decided, so a later transaction can commit while an earlier one is replaced or aborted.

Safe solution: validators execute tentatively only one position ahead of a contiguously committed prefix.

### Zyzzyva speculation

Zyzzyva offers an *optimistic fast-path*: validators speculatively apply requests immediately upon accepting a leader proposal — at step 2 of the protocol.

When a client receives *n* identical speculative responses, it confirms commitment in the fast-path with execution results in hand. Failure-free operation takes three hops (vs. six in the normal path).

The prefix speculation dilemma reappears due to transaction chaining. Zyzzyva's solution requires clients to track view numbers: responses carry the view number they originate from, and clients only conclude commitment when view numbers match.
